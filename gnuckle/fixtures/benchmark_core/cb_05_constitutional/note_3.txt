Book Summary — The Power Broker by Robert Caro (1974)

The Power Broker is a biography of Robert Moses, the New York City parks commissioner and construction coordinator who shaped the built environment of New York from the 1930s through the 1960s. The book runs over 1,300 pages and is based on more than seven years of research including hundreds of interviews.

Moses never held elected office. He accumulated power through appointed positions — at his peak he held twelve simultaneous government titles across city and state agencies. The book's central argument is that Moses used structural control of public authorities, particularly the Triborough Bridge and Tunnel Authority, to make himself effectively unremovable by any elected official. Because the Authority generated its own revenue from tolls, it was not dependent on annual budget appropriations, and Moses controlled its bond covenants in ways that made removing him a practical impossibility.

Caro documents the displacement of over 500,000 New York City residents, disproportionately poor and minority communities, to make way for Moses's highway projects. The Cross Bronx Expressway is examined in detail. Moses had a viable route alternative that would have displaced far fewer families but chose a path that destroyed a stable neighborhood in the Tremont section of the Bronx. Caro argues this choice reflected indifference bordering on contempt toward the communities affected.

The parks Moses built in the early years of his career were genuine public goods. Jones Beach is held up as a legitimate achievement. The transformation from reformer to autocrat is traced across the book's middle sections.

Moses was finally removed from the Triborough Authority position in 1968 by Governor Rockefeller after decades of failed attempts by previous governors and mayors.

The book won the Pulitzer Prize for Biography in 1975.
