Movie Review — Dune: Part Two (2024)

Directed by Denis Villeneuve. Starring Timothee Chalamet, Zendaya, Rebecca Ferguson, Josh Brolin, Austin Butler, Florence Pugh. Rated PG-13. Runtime 166 minutes.

Dune: Part Two picks up immediately after the first film, with Paul Atreides and his mother Jessica embedded with the Fremen of the southern desert. The film covers roughly the second half of Frank Herbert's 1965 novel, culminating in Paul's assumption of leadership and the beginning of his holy war.

Villeneuve made significant changes to the source material. The most notable is the compression and reframing of Paul and Chani's relationship. In the book Chani is largely a believer in the Atreides legend; in the film she is a skeptic throughout, which creates a more dramatically functional arc and gives Zendaya substantially more to do than in the first film.

Austin Butler as Feyd-Rautha is a strong addition. The Harkonnen arena sequence, shot entirely in black-and-white infrared, is visually distinctive and provides contrast to the amber-and-sand palette of the Arrakeen desert.

The film's pacing is denser than Part One. The political exposition that the first film spread across its runtime is compressed here, and viewers unfamiliar with the book may find some of the motivation for secondary characters thin. The Bene Gesserit subplot involving Lady Margot and Feyd-Rautha is abbreviated but functional.

Greig Fraser's cinematography is consistent with the first film. The sandworm riding sequence delivers on the promise of Part One. Hans Zimmer's score continues the synthetic percussive approach established earlier.

The ending is deliberately unresolved. A third film covering Dune Messiah has been confirmed.
